From the BLKOUT picnic archive
The picnic is called. Sunday 16 August, Regent's Park.
Last month we said guard the date. Now you have the place: Regent's Park, 1–7.30pm, one long easy afternoon. No stage. No line-up. No tickets. Just us — and everything we bring.
This year the picnic is built as a constellation of crews. Bring your crew, bring your sound, bring your dominoes and your debate. Come with sixty people or come alone and leave with friends. The first Pride was a picnic — a blanket on the grass is still one of the most radical places a Black queer man can rest. Want to see your own constellation before the day? Play What’s in your sky? — two minutes, stars only, no names.
Everything you need is on one page — what to bring, when, and a “save my spot” box: drop your email and we'll send the exact corner of the park (and a weather word) before the day. The page is made for one job: dropping into the group chat. That's the whole organising model. You, plus the people you love, plus a pin on a map.

Room on the board — this is the call
We trailed this last month; here it is. BLKOUT is a Community Benefit Society — the members own it, and a board of members steers it. Every function works in pairs, for accountability and mutual support, so the work doesn't stall when life happens to one person.
This summer we're recruiting the pair who'll lead on fundraising and finance. In practice that means: keeping our money story honest and legible to members, shaping budgets that match our values, and holding relationships with funders as partnerships rather than begging letters. You don't need to be an accountant. You need to care about this community, be curious about how money moves, and be willing to learn alongside the rest of us — the board learns together, on purpose.
The commitment is honest too: a few hours a month, a monthly board meeting, and a partner in the work so it never sits on you alone. If any of this stirs something — even a maybe — write to rob@blkoutuk.com by the end of July. What follows is a conversation, not a CV sift.
 
After the rainbows: lives are being lost in the gap
Pride month ends; the argument doesn't. For Runnymede Trust's Race Matters blog, Rob wrote about the gap where race-equality work and queer health meet — and too often miss each other. Race equality has learned to take the race–gender intersection seriously; sexuality and gender identity still wait at the door. Meanwhile the health data tells us plainly who falls through.
The health inequalities faced by queer people of the global majority are issues of racial justice. Not adjacent to the mission — the mission.
