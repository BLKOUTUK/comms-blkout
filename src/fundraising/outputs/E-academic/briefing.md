**Methodological Novelty and the Epistemic Void**

In the UK mental health system, Black queer men do not simply face a service deficit; they occupy a profound epistemic void. Clinical practice and diagnostic frameworks routinely misread the compounded impacts of anti-Black racism, intra-community prejudice, and systemic exclusion as individual pathology [1]. The official evidence frequency is similarly mistuned: no dataset in England links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation, and the 2021 Census failed to capture this intersectional population [2-4]. When a population is invisible to the formal archive, the community itself must operate as a hidden healthcare workforce, preventing crises that receive no systemic investment [2, 5].

To understand the scale of this epistemic failure, we have constructed a triangulated, indicative estimate of Mental Health Act detentions. By synthesising population shares, ethnicity-based detention multipliers, and LGBTQ+ crisis vulnerability ratios, our analysis suggests that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained under the Act annually in England [6]. This represents an estimated annual cost to the NHS of £12–16 million [7]. It is epistemically crucial to preserve the strict caveats of this calculation: the estimate is fragile because the required direct data linkage does not exist; it must account for systemic under-disclosure of sexuality; it cannot map the complex, non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination; it does not account for geographic variation; and it must factor in profound trust barriers that prevent early clinical presentation [8, 9]. 

However, the primary theoretical and methodological contribution of Critical Frequency is not merely documenting this data void, but operationalising a genuinely novel intervention in response. Historically, standard care has intervened at the wrong unit of analysis—assuming a patient walks in alone, presents alone, and heals alone [10]. We are applying operational network science, specifically complex contagion theory, to mental health intervention [11, 12]. 

Behaviours that carry significant social risk—such as engaging with therapy or trusting a hostile statutory system—do not spread like a simple virus [12]. They require social reinforcement to traverse a population. Network science demonstrates that complex change spreads most effectively through 'small-world' topologies: tight local clusters connected by wide relational bridges [12, 13]. This precisely mirrors the structure of Black queer community life in the UK [13]. By strategically resourcing 1,825 culturally competent therapy interventions for individuals chosen specifically for their topological position as peripheral connectors and wide bridges, we establish more than 45,000 network exposure points [11, 12, 14]. This mathematically grounded mechanism is designed to push a community of roughly 35,000 past the 25 per cent tipping point, establishing new collective norms of wellness [12].

**The Architecture of Intervention and the Action-Learning Frame**

To sustain this methodological intervention, we have designed an integrated architecture of six interlocking pieces. Crucially, this architecture is community-led from the start; we categorically refuse to adopt models that are merely "designed with community input" whilst retaining centralised, institutional authority. 

1. **Critical Frequency (The Spine):** The central £10.5 million programme operationalising the network-positioned therapy, twelve systems change leaders embedded across statutory institutions, and ten community-led deliberative assemblies producing democratically legitimate policy [11, 15].
2. **Seen/Scene:** A fortnightly digital and live curation space providing the psychic medicine of cultural recognition [16].
3. **The Channel:** A broadcast layer redistributing UK Black queer creator content on high-traffic platforms using counter-algorithmic editorial logic [17].
4. **The Commons:** A shared, community-controlled archive and openly licensed legal framework [18]. 
5. **The Advisory Circle:** A standing circle of paid community organisers holding real authority over the practice [19].
6. **Free Event Ticketing:** Tangible solidarity infrastructure removing platform extraction fees for community organisers [20].

When presented with this architecture, potential academic partners rightly raise questions concerning methodological rigour, research ethics, and intellectual property. Our response is structural. In traditional research paradigms, ethics is frequently treated as an institutional gate to be cleared. In Critical Frequency, ethics is permanently held by community governance [19, 21]. BLKOUT Creative Ltd is constituted as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) with a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [21]. We will not soften this structural commitment to non-extraction: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment and cannot be captured as private wealth [21]. Furthermore, ethical oversight of the practice is held by the Advisory Circle, ensuring that authority is distributed beyond the core organisation [19].

Rigour is maintained by holding the partnership within a strict action-learning frame [19, 22, 23]. Because we are addressing complex, intergenerational systemic failures, we must hold our genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as our primary theoretical strengths. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept; community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale; and the interaction between artificial intelligence, ancestral intelligence, and community trust remains uncharted territory [23]. We refuse to soften these realities into a "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative epistemological design questions that demand rigorous scholarly partnership [23]. Consequently, we will not convert this action-learning frame into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [19, 22]. Academic partners will participate alongside the community in transparently examining the work as it evolves, without overriding the community's governance [22].

**Uncharted Territory: Community-Led Data Stewardship and IP**

For intellectual property and digital rights scholars, the most critical piece of this architecture is The Commons [18]. This serves as the digital substrate and community-controlled archive of Black queer British cultural memory [18].

We are navigating uncharted territory in community-led data stewardship, where the imperative to gather longitudinal evidence must be balanced against the justified refusal of extractive surveillance. Through The Commons, we are commissioning and drafting an openly licensed fair-use and image-rights framework, alongside cooperative governance documentation [18]. This ensures that the architecture we build becomes shared sector property [18]. Any peer organisation across the structurally excluded sector will be able to adopt these frameworks, templates, and archive protocols without repeating the costly legal research [18]. We are actively seeking scholars of IP, data ethics, and digital rights to partner with us in drafting these openly licensed protocols, creating a rigorous, replicable model for community data sovereignty [18, 24].

**Reparations and the Cornerstone Commitment**

We are epistemically and morally explicit about the nature of this proposition. This intervention is anchored in the framework of UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation) [25]. This resolution establishes unequivocally that gross violations of human rights generate binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [25]. 

When mental health disparities emerge as the direct result of colonialism’s ongoing violence, racialised policing, systematic clinical neglect, and cultural erasure, the resourcing of this community’s healing is not charity [25]. We categorically refuse the softening of this reality into the language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy". This is debt repayment [25, 26]. A partner engaging in this work is participating in a serious reparative project with shared standing, building the infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [26, 27].

To underwrite the integration of this architecture, we are seeking a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years, structured in milestones [28, 29]. We deliberately will not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders for the foundational tranche; complex systems change requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor [28, 30]. This commitment represents 40–60 per cent of the total architecture cost and serves as the definitive catalyst to unlock the wider resourcing landscape [22, 28]. 

**The Evaluation Framework and Scholarly Gain**

For medical sociologists, public health institutes, and race-and-health scholars, the evaluation framework embedded in our phased partnership model provides unprecedented research access. The programme initiates with a tightly specified Foundation Year (Year 1), requiring £250,000 to £500,000 [31, 32]. This foundation phase is explicitly dedicated to establishing the baseline research framework, confirming the evaluation design, gathering pre-intervention data, and convening the Advisory Circle [31-34]. 

This ensures that when full delivery commences in Years 2–5—rolling out the 1,825 network-positioned interventions and ten deliberative assemblies—the metrics required to track the longitudinal data set are already operationalised [13, 34, 35]. 

For academic and research partners, the intellectual gains of this collaboration are substantial. You will be participating in the generation of the first community-led longitudinal mental-health dataset on this intersectional population in the UK [36, 37]. You will gain access to a research repository that will become the UK’s deepest community-owned evidence base for Black men’s mental health [11, 24]. Above all, you will serve as co-authors of the evaluation framework and co-publishers of the methodological learnings, proving the efficacy of complex contagion applied to structural health inequalities [22, 38]. 

We possess the cooperative legal home, the historical lineage, and the mapped network science [21, 30, 39]. We are now inviting rigorous, epistemically careful academic partners to help us examine, evaluate, and share what happens when a community retunes the system.
