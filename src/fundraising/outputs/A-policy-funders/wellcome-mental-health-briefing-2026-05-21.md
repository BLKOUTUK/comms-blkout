**The Economics of System Failure and the Data Gap**

The UK mental health system is operating with a structural data void regarding Black queer men—a gap that must be understood not as an administrative oversight, but as a profound system failure [1, 2]. In the contemporary public health landscape, populations that cannot be quantified are systematically excluded from proactive investment. Currently, no official dataset in England links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation, and the 2021 Census entirely failed to capture this intersectional demographic [1, 2]. Invisible to the diagnostic and evidence frequencies of the clinical establishment, the community operates as a hidden healthcare workforce, with peer networks managing severe crises that receive no systemic investment [1, 3]. 

The prevention economics are stark. While NHS talking-therapy completion rates for Black ethnic groups sit at an abysmal 3.8 per cent, the coercive end of the system operates with devastating efficiency [4]. Black people are detained under the Mental Health Act at four times the rate of their white peers [5]. Triangulated analysis—combining ONS population data, NHS Digital Mental Health Act statistics, and LGBTQ+ crisis multipliers—suggests that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained annually in England [1, 6]. This equates to an estimated annual cost to the NHS of £12–16 million for this demographic alone [1, 7]. 

It is epistemically necessary to approach these statistics with rigorous intellectual honesty, preserving their original caveats. This estimate is fragile and strictly indicative precisely because the required direct data linkage does not exist [8]. The calculation relies on conservative population estimates that factor in systemic under-disclosure of sexuality [8, 9]; it cannot perfectly map the complex, non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination [8]; it does not account for geographic variation [10]; and it must factor in the severe trust barriers that prevent early clinical presentation [10]. Nevertheless, the economic reality it reveals is unassailable: a single year of coercive, acute crisis response for this population (£12–16 million) eclipses the entire £10.5 million cost of the five-year prevention infrastructure we are proposing [3, 7]. 

When presented with this intervention, policy funders rightly ask: *"Show me the evidence this works."* Our response is uncompromising: building the evidence base *is* the work. Standard mental health research historically extracts from this community, leading to a collapse in trust that is itself a barrier to evidence-building [11]. The existing data infrastructure simply does not see this population [1]. Network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept, and we hold this reality as a strength [12]. The highly specified Foundation Year is precisely where the baseline research framework and evaluation design are built and confirmed before full-scale delivery commences [13-15].

**The Methodological Advance: Operational Network Science**

Standard mental health care assumes a patient walks in alone, presents alone, and is treated alone [16]. It intervenes at the wrong unit of analysis [16]. Critical Frequency recognises that the actual mechanism of change—and of distress—is networked [16, 17]. 

Our architecture replaces the flawed individualised model with operational network science, specifically drawing on complex contagion theory [18, 19]. Complex behaviours that carry high social risk—such as acknowledging vulnerability, seeking therapy, or trusting a system known to be hostile—do not spread like a simple virus [17, 19]. They require robust social reinforcement to traverse a population [17]. Network science demonstrates that these behaviours spread most effectively through 'small-world' topologies—tight local clusters connected by wide relational bridges—when exposure to the new behaviour crosses a critical threshold of approximately 25 per cent [17, 19]. Black queer community life in the UK exactly mirrors this topographical structure [17]. 

Critical Frequency operationalises this science by deliberately funding 1,825 culturally competent therapy recipients over five years [15, 18, 20]. These individuals are selected not merely for their clinical need, but for their structural position within community clusters as peripheral connectors and wide bridges [18, 21]. By equipping these specific nodes, the 1,825 recipients function as more than 45,000 network exposure points [19, 20]. This is a mathematically grounded mechanism designed to push a population of roughly 35,000 men past the 25 per cent tipping point, establishing new collective norms of wellness and help-seeking [19, 20]. By design, every element of this modern architecture draws on a lived precedent from seven historical Black queer lives, proving the methodology for transformation has long existed within the lineage itself [22, 23].

To ensure systemic translation, this spine is reinforced by twelve systems change leaders embedded across NHS trusts and social care to reform practice from within, and ten community-led deliberative assemblies producing democratically legitimate policy [15, 17, 18]. 

**Reparations and Structural Non-Extraction**

We are explicit about the moral and economic nature of this proposition. This intervention is anchored in the framework of UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation, 2005) [23]. The resolution establishes unequivocally that gross violations of human rights generate binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [23]. 

When mental health disparities emerge as the direct, systemic result of colonialism’s ongoing violence, racialised policing, systematic clinical neglect, and cultural erasure, the resourcing of healing cannot be categorised as charity [23]. We categorically refuse the softening of this reality into the language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy" [23, 24]. This is debt repayment [23]. A funder entering this space is participating in a serious reparative project with shared standing, building the vital public health infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [24, 25].

To ensure this infrastructure remains permanently non-extractive, BLKOUT Creative Ltd has been registered as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) with the UK Financial Conduct Authority [26]. This structure holds a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [26]. We will not soften this structural commitment: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment; it cannot be captured or accumulated as private wealth [26]. Furthermore, this architecture is community-led from the start, a stance we definitively refuse to reframe as being merely "designed with community input" whilst retaining centralised, institutional authority [26].

**Holding Genuine Uncertainty: The Action-Learning Frame**

Because we are addressing complex, intergenerational systemic failures, we must hold our genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as our primary methodological strengths [12]. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept [12]. Community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale [12]. The intersection between artificial intelligence, network mapping, and community trust remains uncharted territory [12]. We absolutely refuse to soften these realities into a "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative design questions that make this partnership essential [12]. 

Consequently, our partner must be prepared to engage with us in a rigorous action-learning frame [12, 27]. We will not convert this transparent practice of collective inquiry into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [27, 28]. The partner will sit alongside our Advisory Circle to transparently examine the work as it evolves, co-authoring the evaluation framework without steering the fundamental direction of the community's governance [27-29].

**The Proposition: A Single Cornerstone Partner and The Foundation Year**

We are proposing a deep, multi-year commitment to underwrite the infrastructure of a movement, not the delivery of an isolated project [30]. We are seeking a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years [31, 32]. We will deliberately not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders to meet this initial threshold; complex systems change requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor [30, 31]. This singular commitment, representing 40–60 per cent of the integrated architecture's total cost, acts as the catalyst required to unlock the remainder of the diversified resourcing landscape [31, 32]. 

The partnership initiates with a tightly specified Foundation Year (Year 1), requiring an initial commitment of £250,000 to £500,000 [13, 14, 33]. This allocation is strictly not a preparatory phase; it is the concrete construction of the trust, operational tooling, and evidence frameworks upon which the scale of delivery entirely depends [14, 33]. 

This foundational £250,000–£500,000 tranche actively unlocks:
*   The immediate hiring and embedding of the Programme Director [13, 14, 33].
*   The completion of rigorous community consultation and co-design listening events across UK networks [13, 14, 33].
*   The recruitment and clinical training of the culturally competent therapist network prior to patient intake [13, 15, 33].
*   The technical specification and procurement readiness of the digital platform [13, 15, 33].
*   The establishment of the baseline research framework and evaluation design, securing the metrics required to track the longitudinal data set [13, 15, 33].
*   The convening of the Advisory Circle and the formalisation of the wider partner coalition [13, 15, 33].

By the close of Year 1, the architecture will be fully operationalised, allowing the £4–6 million cornerstone commitment to securely support full-scale delivery in Years 2–5 [15, 33]. 

Ultimately, this investment replaces a £12–16 million annual failure of coercive state detention with a £10.5 million, five-year architecture of community-led prevention [3, 7]. The cooperative legal home is secured, and the network science is mapped [34]. We are now inviting the right partner to participate in retuning the system [35].
