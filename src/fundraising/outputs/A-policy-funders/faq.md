**EVIDENCE**

**1. Where is the existing evidence that this specific intervention works?**
Building the evidence base *is* the work. Standard mental health research historically extracts from communities, leading to a collapse in trust that is now a barrier to evidence-building [1, 2]. Furthermore, no official dataset in England links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation, and the 2021 Census entirely failed to capture this intersectional population [3, 4]. Network-positioned therapy is a genuinely novel clinical concept; there is no existing dataset for it [5]. We hold this uncertainty as a strength. The £250,000–£500,000 Foundation Year is specifically designed to establish the baseline research framework and confirm the evaluation design before large-scale delivery begins [6, 7]. 

**2. How can you claim a £12–16 million detention cost without official data linking detention to sexual orientation?**
Because the data gap is itself a system failure, we have produced a triangulated indicative estimate [4]. Black people are detained at four times the rate of white peers [8]. Applying conservative to upper-bound multipliers (1.5x to 2.5x) for documented LGB+ crisis risks, we estimate 150–200 Black queer men are detained annually under the Mental Health Act [9, 10]. At an average cost of £78,000 per detention, this costs the NHS £12–16 million annually [10, 11]. We preserve the caveats: this estimate is fragile because the direct data linkage does not exist, and it cannot perfectly map non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination or geographic variation [12, 13]. 

**3. What measurable outcomes will this programme generate by 2031?**
By 2031, we will deliver the UK’s first community-led longitudinal mental-health dataset on this population, evidencing a measurable decrease in severe crisis [14, 15]. You will also see a standing cultural infrastructure, twelve systems change leaders embedded across the NHS and policy sectors, and an openly licensed governance framework adopted by peer organisations [14-16]. We are building the UK's deepest community-owned evidence base for Black men's mental health, turning isolated healing into a measurable, infrastructural reality [5, 16]. 

**4. How will the evaluation framework avoid the extractive practices of past mental health research?**
Mental health research in the UK historically extracts from communities, pathologising individuals to produce scholarship written elsewhere [1, 2]. Our evaluation methodology sits within 'The Commons'—a shared digital infrastructure and community-controlled archive [17, 18]. Ethical oversight and practice are held by our Advisory Circle, a standing body of paid community organisers [19, 20]. By embedding the evaluation within a two-year action-learning programme led by peer organisers, we ensure that the knowledge produced is owned by the community, serving their needs rather than extracting data for external consumption [16, 19].

**MECHANISM**

**5. What is 'network-positioned therapy' and how does it differ from standard clinical provision?**
Standard services intervene at the wrong unit of analysis; they assume a patient walks in alone and is treated alone [2, 21]. But the actual mechanism of change in Black queer life is networked [2, 21]. Network-positioned therapy strategically targets 1,825 culturally competent therapy recipients chosen for their topological position as peripheral connectors and wide bridges within trusted community clusters [22-24]. They are not 1,825 isolated individuals; they act as more than 45,000 network exposure points, mathematically designed to tip a community of roughly 35,000 into new collective norms of wellness [23, 25].

**6. What if complex contagion theory doesn't cleanly transfer to mental health interventions?**
Complex contagion theory demonstrates that behaviours requiring high social reinforcement—such as seeking therapy—spread effectively through 'small-world' networks when exposure crosses a roughly 25 per cent threshold [23, 25]. Black queer community life exactly mirrors this tight-cluster, wide-bridge topology [23, 26]. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept, and its translation to mental health is uncharted territory [5]. We absolutely refuse to soften this into a "tested methodology"—it is a genuine uncertainty that we hold as our greatest methodological strength, tested transparently through our action-learning frame [5].

**7. Why not just fund a mass media public health campaign?**
The system is broadcasting on the wrong frequency [27, 28]. Standard public health campaigns fail to alter entrenched, stigmatised behaviours because complex contagion does not spread like a simple virus [25]. It requires repeated exposure through trusted ties [24]. Funding an ad campaign simply keeps trying to tune the listener; our architecture tunes the broadcast [21]. By positioning individuals across community networks and reinforcing those new norms through our counter-algorithmic broadcast layer (The Channel), we create the social reinforcement required to actually shift deep-rooted behaviours [22, 24, 29].

**8. How do the systems change leaders and deliberative assemblies translate community healing into policy change?**
Healing in isolation does not alter the structural conditions of distress. Therefore, the spine of our architecture includes twelve systems change leaders embedded part-time across NHS trusts, social care, and housing associations to reform practice from the inside [22, 30, 31]. Alongside them, ten deliberative assemblies will generate policy recommendations with democratic legitimacy [22, 26]. We also ask assembly partners to co-invest financially, which builds shared ownership of the policy outcomes and ensures the margins don't just inform the centre, they actively reform it [31, 32].

**COST**

**9. Why are you seeking a single £4–6 million cornerstone partner rather than assembling a panel of contributing funders?**
We are proposing a deep, multi-year commitment to underwrite the infrastructure of a movement, not the delivery of an isolated project [27, 33]. We will categorically not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders. Complex systems change requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor [34, 35]. This £4–6 million cornerstone commitment—representing 40–60 per cent of the total architecture cost—is the necessary catalyst to unlock the remainder of the diversified resourcing landscape, including NHS partnerships and public-health commissioning [36-38]. 

**10. You frame this as 'reparations' rather than social investment. What does that mean practically for a funder?**
We ground this work strictly in UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147, which establishes obligations to restore and compensate for gross human rights violations [39, 40]. When mental health disparities are the direct result of colonialism’s ongoing violence, racialised policing, and cultural erasure, resourcing healing is debt repayment [39, 40]. We will not soften this into "social investment" or "impact philanthropy". Practically, this means a funder is participating in a reparative project with shared standing, refusing deficit framing, and building the public health infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [41-43].

**11. What exactly does the £250,000–£500,000 Foundation Year pay for?**
The £250,000–£500,000 Foundation Year is explicitly not a preparatory phase; it is the concrete construction of the trust and operational tooling upon which the £9.2 million delivery phase entirely depends [6, 7, 44]. It actively unlocks the hiring and embedding of the Programme Director, comprehensive community consultation listening events, the recruitment and training of the culturally competent therapist network, the technical specification of the digital platform, and the convening of the Advisory Circle [6, 7, 31, 44]. By year-end, the architecture is fully operationalised [7].

**GOVERNANCE**

**12. How do you guarantee the community actually retains control, rather than just 'consulting' them?**
This architecture is community-led from the start, a structural reality we unequivocally refuse to reframe as being merely "designed with community input". BLKOUT Creative Ltd is a legally constituted Community Benefit Society (CBS) where over 200 active members hold voting rights to govern our practice [45, 46]. Furthermore, we have established an Advisory Circle of paid community organisers from across the structurally excluded sector [19, 20]. This circle holds real, independent authority over the action-learning curriculum and the openly licensed governance documentation, ensuring power is permanently distributed [19, 20].

**13. We usually require a steering group for a grant of this size. Why do you refuse this structure?**
We will strictly not convert our action-learning frame into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [5, 37]. Steering groups exist to manage risk by enforcing predetermined, tested methodologies. Because we are addressing intergenerational systemic failures, we hold genuine uncertainties as our primary methodological strengths [5]. We invite you to sit alongside our Advisory Circle to transparently examine the work as it evolves, without overriding community governance [37, 47]. You participate in the rigorous examination of the practice, rather than steering its fundamental direction [37].

**14. What does the CBS asset-lock mean for the intellectual property and data generated by this work?**
Our Community Benefit Society carries a permanent, unalterable asset-lock registered with the FCA [43, 45]. We will never soften this structural commitment to non-extraction: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment and cannot be captured as private wealth [43, 45]. Consequently, the IP generated—including fair-use frameworks, cooperative governance templates, and archive protocols—is placed into 'The Commons' and openly licensed [17, 20]. This ensures it becomes shared sector property, allowing peer organisations to adopt these tools without repeating costly legal research [17, 20].

**RISK**

**15. What happens if Rob Berkeley (the founder) leaves or transitions out of the organisation?**
Ten years of trust-building has intentionally culminated in an architecture designed to outlast its founder [45, 48]. The CBS cooperative structure distributes legal governance to a board of seven directors and 200+ voting members [45, 46]. Operationally, the Foundation Year explicitly funds the hiring of a dedicated Programme Director to manage delivery [6, 44]. Finally, Years 6 and 7 are deliberately allocated for consolidation, methodological documentation, and succession planning [49]. The ultimate legacy is a standing infrastructure inheritable by the membership and the wider diaspora [34, 49].

**16. Community-led data stewardship and AI introduce significant data privacy risks. How are these mitigated?**
We are navigating uncharted territory. Community-led data stewardship at this scale is unproven, and the intersection between machine learning, ancestral intelligence, and community trust presents profound ethical questions [5]. We absolutely refuse to soften these realities into "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative design questions that demand an action-learning approach [5]. We mitigate this by housing the data inside The Commons, a community-controlled archive [17, 18]. Stewardship protocols are developed collaboratively with peer organisers through a two-year Weaving Liberation Digital Justice fund programme, ensuring ethics are integrated into the method via informed consent [19].

**17. What if the network theory fails and the 25% tipping point is not reached?**
If the complex contagion effect underperforms, the baseline structural investment still delivers unprecedented clinical and cultural value. The programme secures 1,825 fully funded, culturally competent therapy interventions for an excluded demographic [22, 31]. It embeds twelve systems change leaders across statutory institutions [22, 31], launches ten deliberative assemblies [22, 26], and builds a massive, openly licensed cultural archive [17, 50]. The action-learning frame exists precisely to measure, document, and adapt the contagion effects in real time, guaranteeing that the learnings generated are rigorous and transparent [5, 34].

**EXIT**

**18. If we fund the £4–6 million cornerstone tranche, what happens at the end of the five-to-seven-year period?**
Years 6 and 7 are explicitly dedicated to consolidation, evaluation, and succession [49]. The exit strategy is inheritance. You are investing to become an architect of succession, leaving behind a standing cultural infrastructure, a community-owned evidence base, and an openly licensed cooperative model that will outlast the funding [14, 34]. The cornerstone commitment unlocks the diversified resourcing landscape required to sustain the work long-term, including NHS partnerships, public-health commissioning, and community membership income [36]. 

**19. How does this model scale or transfer to other demographics beyond Black queer men in the UK?**
The architecture is purposefully designed as a transferable model [5, 51]. By underwriting The Commons, you are funding the creation of openly licensed fair-use frameworks, governance templates, and archive protocols built as shared sector property [17, 20]. By 2031, this model of network-positioned therapy, peer-network infrastructure, and community-owned data stewardship will be fully documented and ready for adoption by peer organisations and other marginalised populations facing parallel system failures across Europe and the diaspora [5, 16, 51].
