Dear Solidarity Fund team,

I'm writing on behalf of BLKOUT Creative Ltd about Critical Frequency, an infrastructure programme for the mental health of Black queer men in the UK.

For the past decade, BLKOUT has been building the UK's largest network for this community. We operate as a hidden healthcare workforce, quietly managing crises that receive no systemic investment, while the NHS spends £12–16 million annually detaining 150–200 of our brothers under the Mental Health Act. One year of this coercive crisis response costs more than the entire five-year prevention infrastructure we are now ready to build.

We are seeking a single cornerstone partner to underwrite this work — a commitment of £4–5 million over five to seven years. Complex systems change requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor to catalyse the wider resourcing landscape.

I want to be clear about what this investment represents. Grounded in UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147, this is an act of institutional restitution for colonialism's ongoing violence, racialised policing, and systemic clinical neglect — not impact philanthropy, not social investment. It is debt repayment: building the infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not. Critical Frequency sits squarely in the territory the Solidarity Fund was made for — racial justice, structural change, community power held at scale.

If you are asking yourself, "Can we trust BLKOUT with a commitment at this scale?", I understand. Our answer lies in ten years of delivery, not a charismatic pitch. In November 2025 we secured this work in a durable cooperative legal home as a Community Benefit Society with a permanent, unalterable asset-lock — structurally, it is legally impossible for this investment to be captured as private wealth. We are community-led from the start, with authority distributed to a paid Advisory Circle of peer organisers.

The partnership opens with a £250,000–£500,000 Foundation Year — concrete construction of the trust, governance and operational tooling on which delivery depends. By the close of Year 1, the architecture is operational, allowing Years 2–5 to roll out 1,825 network-positioned therapy interventions, embedded systems change leaders, and deliberative assemblies.

We are not looking for a transactional funder. We invite you to participate alongside our Advisory Circle in a rigorous action-learning frame — in the room as we examine the work, holding our genuine uncertainties as methodological strengths rather than risks to mitigate.

A slide deck setting out the programme architecture is attached for reference, as FYI rather than required reading. I would value the chance to talk this through with you: a 45-minute conversation to explore whether Critical Frequency belongs in the Solidarity Fund's pipeline.

Warmly,

Rob Berkeley MBE
