**The Clinical Case: System Failure and the Partner Ecology**

Clinical mental health practice in the UK is currently operating with a severe structural blind spot regarding Black queer men. Standard care frequently misreads the compounded impacts of anti-Black racism, intra-community prejudice, and systemic exclusion as individual pathology [1, 2]. The resulting clinical disparities are profound. While NHS Talking Therapies see a 79.8 per cent completion rate for white patients, the completion rate for Black patients sits at an abysmal 3.8 per cent [3]. Simultaneously, the coercive end of the clinical pathway operates with devastating efficiency: Black people are detained under the Mental Health Act at four times the rate of their white peers [4, 5]. 

Because no official dataset in England links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation, this specific intersectional population remains invisible to standard evidence frequencies [2, 6, 7]. Triangulated analysis, however, indicates that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained under the Mental Health Act annually in England, representing a cost to the NHS of £12–16 million [4, 8, 9]. It is strictly necessary to cite these figures with their original, vital caveats intact: this estimate is indicative because the required direct data linkage does not exist; it must account for systemic under-disclosure of sexuality; it cannot fully map the non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination; it does not capture geographic variation; and it must factor in profound trust barriers that prevent early clinical presentation [10, 11]. 

Nevertheless, the cost-avoidance economic case is undeniable. A single year of coercive, acute crisis response for this demographic (£12–16 million) costs the system more than the entire £10.5 million required to fund our proposed five-year prevention infrastructure [4, 9, 12]. 

When reviewing these figures, Integrated Care Board (ICB) commissioners and mental health trust leaders often raise a familiar, valid pushback: *"This isn't a service we can commission as a single line."* We agree entirely. Critical Frequency is not a parallel clinical service competing with NHS provision. It is the necessary partner ecology *around* the statutory service. We are building the trusted intermediary infrastructure that statutory commissioning has historically been unable to build itself. To successfully alter clinical pathways, the ecosystem must be community-led from the start; we categorically refuse to soften this requirement into merely being "designed with community input" [13]. 

**Relational Infrastructure: Retuning the System**

Standard mental health services are designed for individuals; they presume a patient walks in alone, presents alone, and is treated alone [14, 15]. However, the actual mechanism of change—and of distress—within Black queer life is heavily networked [14-16]. When individuals are invisible to formal pathways, the community quietly operates as a hidden healthcare workforce, with peer networks managing crises that would otherwise cost statutory services millions [12, 17]. Critical Frequency is the relational infrastructure designed to properly resource this existing ecology.

Our mechanism is grounded in operational network science, specifically complex contagion theory [18-20]. Behaviours that require significant social reinforcement, such as engaging with therapy or trusting a statutory service known to be hostile, do not spread like a simple virus [18-21]. They spread most effectively through "small-world" network structures—tight local clusters connected by wide relational bridges—when exposure to the new norm crosses a critical threshold of approximately 25 per cent [18, 19, 22, 23]. 

By strategically funding 1,825 culturally competent therapy interventions over five years, we are not simply treating 1,825 isolated individuals [18, 19, 24]. These individuals are selected for their topological position within community clusters, acting as peripheral connectors and wide bridges [19, 24-26]. They function as more than 45,000 network exposure points, mathematically designed to push a population of roughly 35,000 men past the 25 per cent tipping point where individual health-seeking behaviours coalesce into collective cultural norms [18, 19, 27]. This networked therapy functions as a vital service-improvement contribution, acting as the relational primer that makes statutory clinical pathways effective. 

**Embedded Practice: Systems Change Leaders**

To ensure that the intelligence generated by this network translates directly into clinical and public-health equity, Critical Frequency deploys a cohort of twelve systems change leaders [24, 28, 29]. NHS and public-health partners will immediately recognise the value of this embedded-practice model. 

Drawing on the historical precedent of Dr Cecil Belfield Clarke—who reformed medical institutions from the inside for fifty years—these systems change leaders will be embedded part-time across NHS trusts, Integrated Care Systems, social care, and housing associations [24, 28-30]. Crucially, they are supported as a cohort rather than left isolated within statutory bodies [28, 30]. These leaders will work to reform practice from within, bringing lived expertise and community data directly into decision-making rooms [29, 31]. They will be guided by ten deliberative assemblies: community-led policy inquiries that generate democratically legitimate recommendations for public-health integration [22, 24, 29]. Through this architecture, we are not asking for a seat at the clinical table; we are providing the expertise required to redesign it [31].

**Reparations and Structural Non-Extraction**

We are explicit about the moral and economic nature of this proposition. This intervention is anchored in international law, specifically UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation, 2005) [32, 33]. This resolution establishes that gross violations of human rights generate binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [32, 33]. 

When mental health disparities emerge as the direct result of colonialism’s ongoing violence—manifesting through racialised policing, systematic clinical neglect, and a cultural erasure that turns visibility into risk—the resourcing of this community’s healing is not charity [32, 33]. We will strictly not soften this reality into the language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy". This is debt repayment [32, 33]. A partner engaging in this work is participating in a reparative project, building the vital public health infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [34, 35].

To ensure this infrastructure remains permanently non-extractive, BLKOUT Creative Ltd has been constituted as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) registered with the Financial Conduct Authority (Reg. RS009639) [35, 36]. We hold a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [35, 36]. We will not soften this structural commitment to non-extraction: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment and cannot be captured as private wealth [35, 36]. 

**Holding Genuine Uncertainty: The Action-Learning Frame**

Because this work addresses complex, systemic failures, we must hold genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as clinical and methodological strengths. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept; community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale; and the interaction between artificial intelligence, network mapping, and community trust remains uncharted territory [37-39]. We categorically refuse to soften these realities into "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative design questions that make this partnership essential [37, 38]. 

Consequently, we require a partner prepared to engage in a rigorous action-learning frame [37, 40, 41]. We will not convert this practice of collective, transparent inquiry into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [40, 41]. The partner will sit alongside the community's Advisory Circle to transparently examine the work as it evolves, without overriding community governance [40, 41].

**The Proposition and the Partner Coalition Phase**

We are proposing a deep, multi-year commitment to underwrite the infrastructure of a movement, not the delivery of an isolated project [42, 43]. To achieve this, we are seeking a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years, structured in milestones [44, 45]. We will definitively not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders for the foundational tranche. Complex systems integration requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor commitment, which represents 40–60 per cent of the total architecture cost and acts as the catalyst to unlock the wider resourcing landscape, including NHS partnerships and public-health commissioning [41, 44, 45]. 

This partnership initiates with a tightly specified Foundation Year (Year 1), requiring an upfront commitment of £250,000 to £500,000 [46-48]. This is not a preparatory phase; it is the concrete construction of the trust and operational tooling upon which the scale of delivery entirely depends [47, 48]. 

Crucially for NHS and public-health stakeholders, Year 1 is explicitly designed to formalise the partner coalition required for Years 2–5 delivery [29, 47, 48]. This foundational £250,000–£500,000 tranche directly unlocks:
*   The immediate hiring and embedding of the Programme Director [46-48].
*   The completion of rigorous community consultation and co-design listening events to confirm priorities [46-48].
*   The recruitment and clinical training of the culturally competent therapist network prior to patient intake [29, 46, 47].
*   The technical specification and procurement readiness of the digital platform [29, 46, 47].
*   The establishment of the baseline research framework and evaluation design, securing the metrics required to track the longitudinal data set [29, 46, 47].
*   The convening of the Advisory Circle and the launch of the solidarity infrastructure [46, 49].

By the close of Year 1, the architecture will be fully operationalised, allowing the £4–6 million cornerstone commitment to securely underwrite full-scale delivery [29, 49, 50]. This delivery phase will dispatch the twelve embedded systems change leaders, launch the deliberative assemblies, and deploy the 1,825 network-positioned therapy interventions that will retune the system [29, 45, 50]. 

We are not asking to be supported as an external charity. We are offering statutory, clinical, and public-health stakeholders the trusted intermediary infrastructure required to fulfill their own equity mandates. We invite you to participate in retuning the system.
