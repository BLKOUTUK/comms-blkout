**Cultural Memory Infrastructure for an Under-Archived Community**

To understand the architecture of Critical Frequency, one must first confront a profound archival silence. Black queer men in Britain are among the most under-served and under-evidenced populations in the country [1]. We are an under-archived community, navigating a public sphere and a state apparatus that routinely fails to capture our existence. The 2021 Census did not meaningfully record this intersectional population, and no official dataset in England links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation [1, 2]. When a community is invisible to the official archive, it is denied the structural investment required to sustain its cultural memory and its collective wellbeing. 

At this juncture, a pertinent question often arises from cultural funders: how does a proposition concerning mental health outcomes align with the priorities of arts, culture, and heritage funding? Our response is unequivocal: the divisions between mental health, cultural production, and digital infrastructure are the system's sectors, not the community's reality [3]. For a population facing systemic erasure, cultural recognition is not adjacent to mental-health work; it *is* the prevention work [4]. When a community has been pathologised by the clinical establishment, providing spaces of slow attention, dialogue, and cultural reflection offers the psychic medicine of recognition [4, 5]. 

The cost of this ongoing archival and cultural erasure is devastating. Triangulated analysis suggests that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained under the Mental Health Act every year, at an annual cost to the NHS of £12–16 million [1, 6, 7]. It is necessary to cite these figures with their original, vital caveats intact: this estimate is fragile precisely because no direct data linkage exists between detention and sexual orientation; it must account for widespread under-disclosure of sexuality; it cannot map the non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination; it does not capture geographic variation; and it must factor in the profound trust barriers that prevent early clinical presentation [8, 9]. Yet, this indicative data reveals a stark reality: a single year of coercive state crisis response eclipses the entire £10.5 million cost of the five-year cultural and preventative infrastructure we propose [7]. 

**Methodology Embodied: The Seven Lives**

Critical Frequency does not invent a new methodology for community survival; it formalises the one that has always existed. By design, every element of our programme is anchored in a lived precedent from Black queer British and diasporic history [10, 11]. We view these historical figures not merely as archival subjects, but as the rigorous methodological blueprint for our cultural infrastructure [12, 13]. The lineage itself is the architecture [12]. 

*   **Julius Caesar Taylor (1720s):** Taylor ran a molly house on Tottenham Court Road, a space where queer men found each other in the shadow of capital punishment [10]. He provides the precedent for our **social interventions**, proving that finding each other in spaces of cultural safety is the first act of resistance [10, 11].
*   **Dr Cecil Belfield Clarke (1914):** One of Britain’s first Black doctors, who reformed institutions from the inside over fifty years at his Elephant and Castle practice [14]. He anchors our cohort of **twelve systems change leaders**, who will be embedded across cultural policy and public health institutions to reform practice from within, supported rather than isolated [14, 15].
*   **Ivor Cummings (1941):** Known as the “gay father of the Windrush generation,” Cummings organised the funeral for his friend Ken "Snakehips" Johnson after the Café de Paris bombing, holding the community together when systems failed [14]. He is the foundation for our **ten deliberative assemblies**, demonstrating that the gathering itself is the infrastructure, and care is the work [11, 14].
*   **Berto Pasuka (1946):** Founder of Les Ballets Nègres, who famously rebuked a critic’s demand for classical conformity by asking if ice-cream is worthless because it is not cheese [16]. Pasuka’s legacy is embedded in **The Channel**, our curatorial broadcast layer which redistributes Black queer creator content, affirming that our healing and our art do not need to look like anyone else’s to be effective [16, 17].
*   **Alvin Baltrop (1975):** The photographer who documented life on the abandoned Hudson piers, trusted by every subject he captured [16]. Baltrop anchors our **evaluation methodology**, proving that the most rigorous, ethical evidence comes solely from those trusted enough to be let in [13, 16].
*   **Joseph Beam (1984):** The editor who compiled the first anthology of Black gay writing because the shelves were empty, placing an advert stating "Visibility is survival" [18]. Beam is the spirit of our **research repository** and cultural curation room, *Seen/Scene*, reinforcing that when you create the archive, you create the community [5, 18]. 
*   **Justin Fashanu (1998):** The first Black footballer to come out, found dead in a Shoreditch garage [18]. Fashanu’s tragic trajectory necessitates our **network-positioned therapy programme**: an infrastructure built so that no one else is hyper-visible to the world yet invisible to the systems that should have kept them alive [18]. 

Through courts, critics, and systems, each of these lives was historically framed as a problem—as disorder, voyeurism, or amateurism [12, 13]. Our curatorial act is to reframe those exact patterns as the definitive methodology for transformation [12]. 

**The Rigorous Mechanism Beneath the Culture**

Beneath this deeply cultural and archival work lies a rigorous mechanism grounded in operational network science [19, 20]. The spread of complex behaviours—such as seeking help, sharing one's story, or trusting a system—does not operate like a simple viral broadcast [21, 22]. It requires social reinforcement, or "complex contagion," to traverse a population [21, 22]. 

Black queer community life in the UK forms a "small-world" network structure: tight local clusters connected by wide relational bridges [21, 23]. By strategically resourcing 1,825 individuals positioned as peripheral connectors and wide bridges across these clusters, we are establishing more than 45,000 network exposure points [21, 24]. This is mathematically designed to push a community of roughly 35,000 past the 25 per cent tipping point where individual behaviours coalesce into new, collective cultural norms [4, 21]. Network science is the structural engine; the cultural production is the vehicle that carries this retuning across the network.

**Reparations and Structural Non-Extraction**

We are explicit about the moral and economic nature of this proposition. This work is grounded in UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation) [12, 25]. This resolution establishes that gross violations of human rights generate binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [12, 25].

When a community's psychic distress is the direct result of colonialism’s ongoing violence, racialised policing, and cultural erasure, the resourcing of its healing and its archive is not charity [12, 25]. We categorically refuse the softening of this reality into the language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy" [26]. This is debt repayment [12]. A funder stepping into this space is participating in a reparative project, building the cultural memory infrastructure the system was obligated to build, but did not [26, 27].

To ensure this work remains structurally non-extractive, BLKOUT Creative Ltd has been registered as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) with the UK Financial Conduct Authority (Reg. RS009639) [28]. We hold a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [28]. Every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment; it cannot be captured or accumulated as private wealth [11, 28]. Furthermore, this architecture is community-led from the start, a stance we will not soften into merely being "designed with community input" whilst retaining external authority [29]. 

**The Proposition and Holding Uncertainty**

We are seeking a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years to underwrite the integration of this architecture [30, 31]. We will not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders for the founding tranche; complex, trust-based infrastructure requires the unified patience and analytical seriousness of a single anchor [30, 32]. This cornerstone commitment, representing 40–60 per cent of the total cost, will decisively unlock the remainder of the resourcing landscape [30, 33]. 

The partnership initiates with a Foundation Year (Year 1) requiring £250,000 to £500,000 [34, 35]. This is not a preparatory phase, but the concrete construction of the trust, the digital platform specifications, the community consultation, and the recruitment necessary to safely deploy the delivery phase in Years 2–5 [34-36]. 

Crucially, the chosen partner must engage with us in an action-learning frame [33, 37]. We will not convert this process into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [33]. We expect our partner to sit alongside the community's Advisory Circle to transparently examine the work [33]. In doing so, we hold our genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as strengths and vital design questions. We acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel concept; community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale; and the interaction between AI, network mapping, and community trust remains uncharted [37]. We refuse to soften these realities into "tested methodology"—they are precisely why the work demands to be done [37, 38].

**The Commons: Openly-Licensed Sector Property**

Ultimately, the goal of Critical Frequency is not institutional self-preservation, but the creation of an inheritable legacy. The foundation of this architecture is The Commons: a shared digital infrastructure and community-controlled archive of Black queer British cultural memory [39, 40]. 

Through this substrate, we are commissioning and drafting an openly-licensed fair-use and image-rights framework, alongside cooperative governance documentation [39, 41]. This ensures that the structural apparatus we build becomes shared sector property [39, 41]. Any peer organisation across the European Black queer and structurally excluded arts sector will be able to adopt these frameworks, templates, and archive protocols without having to repeat the costly legal and infrastructural research [39, 41]. 

We are not asking to be supported. We are inviting a cornerstone partner to build the cultural memory infrastructure of a community that has always possessed the methodology for its own survival, and is now ready to archive, scale, and share it.
