**The Vision and the Cornerstone Invitation**

The UK mental health system is currently broadcasting on the wrong frequency for Black queer reality [1]. It cannot treat what it cannot see, and it cannot hear what it refuses to recognise [1]. For decades, this community has operated as a hidden healthcare workforce, with peer networks quietly managing and preventing crises that receive no systemic investment [2, 3]. We are not proposing a new clinical service. We are proposing the integrated architecture by which a community that is already healing itself can be properly resourced to do so at the scale and rigour the crisis demands [4]. 

The cost of the current system’s failure is stark. Triangulated analysis suggests that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained under the Mental Health Act annually in England, representing an estimated cost to the NHS of £12–16 million [2, 3, 5, 6]. It is vital to approach these statistics with rigorous intellectual honesty, preserving their original caveats: this estimate is indicative because the required direct data linkage between mental health detention and sexual orientation does not exist [7, 8]. The calculation relies on conservative population estimates that factor in the systemic under-disclosure of sexuality; it cannot perfectly map the complex, non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination; it does not account for geographic variation; and it must factor in profound trust barriers that prevent early clinical presentation [7-9]. Yet the economic reality remains unassailable: a single year of coercive, acute crisis response for this demographic eclipses the entire £10.5 million cost of the five-year prevention infrastructure we are building [3, 6].

To construct this infrastructure, we are seeking a single cornerstone partner with the vision, conviction, and patience for trust-based work [1]. We are proposing a deep, multi-year commitment of £4–6 million over five to seven years [10, 11]. We will deliberately not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders for the founding tranche. Complex systems change requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor [12, 13]. This singular commitment, representing 40–60 per cent of the integrated architecture’s total cost, serves as the definitive catalyst required to unlock the remainder of the diversified resourcing landscape [10-12]. 

**Founder Credibility and Trusting the Scale**

When presented with an intervention of this ambition, it is entirely reasonable to ask: *"Can I trust BLKOUT with a £4–6 million commitment at this scale?"* The answer to that question does not lie in a charismatic founder biography, but in a ten-year track record of operational seriousness and proven delivery [14]. 

Since our founding in 2016, we have built the UK's largest network for Black queer men [14, 15]. Over a decade of practice, we have successfully delivered complex funding, including a £59,000 National Lottery Heritage Fund project [16, 17]. We have cultivated a 200-member community platform, BLKOUTHUB, governed by active peer support and ongoing voting rights [16-18]. We have forged standing relationships with senior figures across UK public health and equality organisations, including OHID London, the NHS Race and Health Observatory, and the Black Equity Organisation [16, 17]. We possess the lineage of trust required to do this work [19]. What we do not yet have—and what we are inviting you to build with us—is the integrated infrastructure that allows this architecture to run at the scale the crisis demands [19].

**The Asset-Lock and Distributed Authority**

To guarantee that your investment and our infrastructure remain structurally non-extractive, we have anchored this decade of practice in a durable cooperative legal home. In November 2025, BLKOUT Creative Ltd was registered as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) with the UK Financial Conduct Authority (Reg. RS009639) [18, 20]. 

Crucially, this legal structure carries a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [18, 20]. We will not soften this structural commitment: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment, making it structurally impossible for this wealth to be captured or accumulated as private profit [18, 20]. There are no external shareholders; the cooperative board comprises seven Black queer-identified directors [17, 18]. 

Furthermore, authority within this architecture is deliberately distributed. We have established an Advisory Circle—a standing body of paid community organisers drawn from across the UK Black queer and structurally excluded movements [21, 22]. This circle holds real, independent authority over the practice, distributing power beyond the core organisation [21, 22]. This is what it means to be community-led from the start, a foundational stance we categorically refuse to soften into merely being "designed with community input" [23]. 

**Genuine Participation: The Action-Learning Frame**

We are looking for an intimacy of engagement that transactional funding models cannot provide. We invite our cornerstone partner to participate alongside the Advisory Circle in a rigorous action-learning frame [12, 24]. You will sit in the room where the work is transparently examined as it evolves, without steering the fundamental direction of the community's governance [12]. We will emphatically not convert this transparent practice of collective inquiry into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [12].

We insist on this action-learning frame because we must hold our genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as our primary methodological strengths [24]. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept [24]. Community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale [24]. The intersection between artificial intelligence, network mapping, and community trust remains uncharted territory [24, 25]. We absolutely refuse to soften these realities into a "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative design questions that make this partnership worthy of your conviction [24]. 

**Reparations, Not Charity**

We are explicit about the economic and moral nature of this proposition. This intervention is anchored in the framework of UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation, 2005) [26, 27]. This resolution establishes unequivocally that gross violations of human rights generate binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [26, 27]. 

When a mental health crisis is the direct, compounded result of colonialism’s ongoing violence—manifesting through the racialised policing of queerness, systemic clinical neglect, and the cultural erasure that turns visibility into a mortal risk—resourcing this community’s healing is not charity [26, 27]. We categorically refuse the softening of this reality into the palatable language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy". This is debt repayment [26, 27]. A cornerstone partner entering this space is not acting as a benefactor; they are participating in a serious reparative project with shared standing, building the vital infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [20, 27-29].

**The Mechanism: Complex Contagion at Scale**

The engine driving this transformation is operational network science. Standard mental health services intervene at the wrong unit of analysis—assuming a patient walks in alone, presents alone, and is treated alone [30, 31]. However, distress travels through relationships, and therefore healing must travel through relationships too [32, 33].

We rely on complex contagion theory, which demonstrates that behaviours carrying social risk—such as engaging with therapy or trusting a hostile statutory system—do not spread like a simple virus [34-36]. They require social reinforcement to traverse a population, spreading most effectively through 'small-world' topologies consisting of tight local clusters connected by wide relational bridges [34, 37, 38]. This mirrors the exact structure of Black queer community life in the UK [37]. By strategically positioning 1,825 culturally competent therapy recipients as peripheral connectors and wide bridges across the network, we establish more than 45,000 network exposure points [34, 37, 39, 40]. This is a mathematically grounded mechanism designed to push a community of roughly 35,000 past the 25 per cent tipping point, establishing new collective norms of wellness [34, 40, 41].

**Legacy: What £4–6 Million Unlocks**

Your cornerstone commitment underwrites a seven-year arc of transformation [10, 11]. The partnership commences with a tightly specified Foundation Year (Year 1), requiring an initial allocation of £250,000 to £500,000 [42-44]. This is not a preparatory phase; it is the concrete construction of the trust and operational capacity upon which the scale of delivery entirely depends [43]. This foundational tranche actively unlocks the immediate hiring of the Programme Director, the rigorous community consultation events across UK networks, the recruitment and training of the culturally competent therapist network, the technical specification of the digital platform, and the establishment of the baseline evaluation framework [42-45]. 

By the close of Year 1, the integrated architecture will be fully operationalised, allowing your commitment to securely underwrite full-scale delivery in Years 2–5 [43, 45, 46]. This delivery phase will roll out the 1,825 network-positioned therapy interventions, embed twelve systems change leaders across NHS trusts and social care to reform practice from within, and launch ten deliberative assemblies to generate democratically legitimate policy [38, 39, 45, 46]. It will simultaneously sustain our cultural broadcast spaces—*Seen/Scene* and *The Channel*—alongside *The Commons*, our openly licensed digital archive of Black queer British cultural memory [46-50].

Years 6 and 7 are dedicated to consolidation, evaluation, and succession [46]. The ultimate legacy of this partnership will be the creation of the first community-led longitudinal mental-health dataset on this population in the UK, evidencing a measurable decrease in severe crisis [51, 52]. You will serve as an architect of succession, leaving behind a standing cultural infrastructure, an openly licensed cooperative governance model adopted across Europe, and a deep, community-owned evidence base that will inform clinical and policy decisions for decades [13, 25, 51-53]. 

The infrastructure is ready to be built. The cooperative legal home is secured. The historical lineage is held, and the network science is mapped [54]. We are now inviting the right partner to participate with us in retuning the system [55].
