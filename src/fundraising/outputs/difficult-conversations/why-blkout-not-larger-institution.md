# why-blkout-not-larger-institution

---

When proposing a £4–6 million foundational investment, it is entirely reasonable to ask why BLKOUT should hold this work rather than a larger, established equity or health organisation. The answer lies in the fundamental mechanism of the crisis: standard clinical and diagnostic frequencies are mistuned precisely because they lack the trust and relational infrastructure required to effectively reach Black queer men [1, 2]. Larger institutions are vital to this ecosystem, and we actively invite them to partner with us on this work [3]. However, the lived relational expertise necessary to drive complex contagion through this specific population cannot be assembled from institutional resources, nor can it be retrofitted into an existing clinical pathway [4, 5]. 

Only BLKOUT can hold the profound trust this work requires. For a decade, we have quietly built the UK’s largest network for Black queer men, operating as a hidden healthcare workforce to prevent crises that would otherwise cost the state millions [6-8]. We possess a lineage of community trust that large institutions simply do not have [9]. 

This decade of organising is secured within a durable cooperative legal home [7]. BLKOUT Creative Ltd is constituted as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) holding a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [10, 11]. We will never soften this structural commitment to non-extraction; it guarantees that every penny is reinvested in community benefit and cannot be captured as private wealth [10, 11]. We are community-led from the start—a structural reality we unequivocally refuse to dilute into merely being "designed with community input." This mandate is operationalised through our 200-strong active member base, who hold standing voting rights to govern our practice [3, 10, 12]. 

Because this architecture addresses systemic failures, we must hold our genuine uncertainties—the novelty of network-positioned therapy, the unproven scale of community-led data stewardship, and the uncharted territory of artificial intelligence and community trust—not as risks to be mitigated, but as our greatest methodological strengths [13]. A large institution’s conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure would crush this innovation. Instead, we require a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years, joining us in a rigorous action-learning frame to transparently examine these questions together [14, 15]. We will not assemble a panel of contributing funders to dilute this analytical seriousness [14].

Ultimately, this work is a mandate of reparations under UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 [16, 17]. When addressing the compounded harms of colonialism, racialised policing, and systemic neglect, restitution cannot be filtered through an institutional proxy [16, 18]. The community must hold the architecture of its own healing [11, 18].
