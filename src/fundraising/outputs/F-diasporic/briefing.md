**A Shared Diasporic Lineage: Building Transnational Substrate**

Every element of the architecture we are building carries a lived precedent from Black queer diasporic history [1]. We do not claim to have invented a new methodology for community survival; we are simply formalising the one our lineage has practiced for centuries [1, 2]. When we build a research repository to counter our archival erasure, we draw directly on the work of Joseph Beam, who gathered the first anthology of Black gay writing in the United States in 1984 under the banner "Visibility is survival" [3]. When we design an evaluation methodology that relies on profound, non-extractive trust, we look to Alvin Baltrop, who documented life on the abandoned Hudson River piers in 1970s New York, trusted by every subject he captured [4]. 

When we curate a community media broadcast platform, we invoke Berto Pasuka, who arrived from Jamaica to found Les Ballets Nègres in London in 1946, insisting that our cultural healing does not need to conform to white, classical standards to be rigorous [4]. And when we embed systems change leaders within statutory institutions, we follow the blueprint of Dr Cecil Belfield Clarke of Barbados, who reformed British medical institutions from the inside over fifty years at his Elephant and Castle practice [5]. 

When peer organisations in Toronto, Lagos, Johannesburg, São Paulo, or Berlin ask, *"Why should we engage with a UK project?"* our response is unequivocal: because the work is already in conversation with yours. We are not attempting to export a model from a British centre to a global periphery. We are contributing a newly formalised structural apparatus to a transnational movement. The lineage is shared, our epistemic erasure is shared, and the methodology we are building is strictly designed for cross-border adoption [1, 6]. 

**The Epistemic Void and the Network Mechanism**

Across the diaspora, our communities navigate public health and state systems that routinely misread the compounded impacts of anti-Black racism, intra-community prejudice, and colonial-era anti-LGBTQ+ laws as individual pathology [7]. In the UK, this manifests as a profound data void: no official dataset links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation, rendering our crises invisible to structural investment [8]. 

By triangulating population shares, ethnicity-based detention multipliers, and LGBTQ+ crisis vulnerability ratios, our analysis suggests that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained under the Mental Health Act annually in England, representing an estimated cost to the NHS of £12–16 million [9, 10]. It is epistemically necessary to cite these figures with their original caveats intact: this estimate is indicative because the required direct data linkage does not exist; it must account for systemic under-disclosure of sexuality; it cannot map the complex, non-linear relationships of compounded discrimination; it does not capture geographic variation; and it must factor in profound trust barriers that prevent early clinical presentation [11, 12]. 

While the state defaults to coercive crisis intervention, standard mental health services intervene at the wrong unit of analysis—assuming a patient walks in alone and heals alone [13]. Critical Frequency replaces this with operational network science, grounded in complex contagion theory [14, 15]. Complex change does not spread like a simple virus; it requires social reinforcement through 'small-world' network structures [15]. By strategically positioning 1,825 culturally competent therapy recipients as peripheral connectors and wide bridges across the network, we establish more than 45,000 exposure points [14-17]. This mechanism is mathematically designed to push a community past the 25 per cent tipping point, establishing new, collective norms of wellness [15, 18].

**The Commons: Adoptable Apparatus and Non-Extraction**

To sustain this network tipping point, we are building an integrated architecture of six interlocking pieces [19]. At the heart of our offer to international peer partners is **The Commons**: the shared digital infrastructure and community-controlled archive of Black queer cultural memory [6]. 

Through The Commons, we are commissioning and drafting an openly-licensed fair-use and image-rights framework, alongside comprehensive cooperative governance documentation [6]. We are building this as shared sector property [6]. Any peer organisation across the diaspora will be able to adopt these legal frameworks, governance templates, and archive protocols without having to repeat the costly infrastructural research [6, 20]. We are doing the structural heavy lifting in the UK so the apparatus can be inherited globally [6]. 

Crucially, this architecture is **community-led from the start** [21, 22]. We categorically reject models that are merely "designed with community input" whilst retaining centralised, institutional authority. To guarantee that this infrastructure remains permanently non-extractive, BLKOUT Creative Ltd has been registered as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) with the UK Financial Conduct Authority (Reg. RS009639) [23]. We hold a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [23]. We will not soften this structural commitment: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment and cannot be captured or accumulated as private wealth [23]. 

**Reparations, Uncertainty, and the Action-Learning Frame**

We are explicit with potential partners about the economic and moral nature of this proposition. This intervention is anchored in international law, specifically UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation) [2]. This resolution establishes unequivocally that gross violations of human rights generate binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [2]. 

When mental health disparities emerge as the direct result of colonialism’s ongoing violence and cultural erasure, the resourcing of this community’s healing cannot be categorised as charity [2]. We categorically refuse the softening of this reality into the language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy" [2]. This is debt repayment [2]. A funder entering this space is participating in a serious reparative project, building the public health and cultural infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [24, 25].

Because we are addressing complex, intergenerational systemic failures, we hold our genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as our greatest methodological strengths. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept; community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale; and the interaction between artificial intelligence, network mapping, and community trust remains uncharted territory [26-28]. We emphatically refuse to soften these realities into a "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative design questions that make this solidarity work essential [26, 27]. 

Consequently, we demand that any partner engages with us in a rigorous action-learning frame [29]. We will absolutely not convert this transparent practice of collective inquiry into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [29, 30]. The partner will sit alongside the community's Advisory Circle to transparently examine the work as it evolves, without overriding the community's governance or steering its fundamental direction [29]. 

**The Cornerstone Proposition and Transnational Adoption**

To execute this, we are seeking a **single cornerstone partner** to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years to underwrite the integration of this architecture [31]. We will deliberately not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders for the foundational tranche; complex systems change requires the analytical seriousness and unified patience of a single anchor [31, 32]. This commitment covers 40–60 per cent of the total architecture cost and serves as the definitive catalyst to unlock the wider resourcing landscape [29, 31]. 

The partnership initiates with a Foundation Year (Year 1) requiring £250,000 to £500,000 [33, 34]. This phase is the concrete construction of the trust, digital platforms, and research baselines upon which the delivery phase in Years 2–5 entirely depends [33, 34]. 

Ultimately, what transnational adoption looks like in practice is a shared, evolving substrate. By underwriting this architecture in the UK, a cornerstone partner is funding the creation of openly licensed cooperative templates, action-learning curricula, and community data stewardship models that are built to travel [6, 20]. By 2031, we expect this transferable model—network-positioned therapy and community-owned evidence—to be actively utilised by structurally excluded populations in other diasporic nodes [26, 35]. 

We possess the cooperative legal home, the network science, and the historical lineage [23, 36, 37]. We invite diasporic peers to participate in examining the learnings, adopting The Commons, and retuning the system alongside us [6, 38].
