# briefing-D-peer-orgs

**Audience: Profile D — Black, queer, equity peer organisations**

---

**The Commons: Shared Sector Property**

For decades, Black, queer, and structurally excluded peer organisations have operated as the hidden infrastructure of our communities. We provide the mutual aid, the spaces of safety, and the crisis intervention that statutory systems fail to deliver [1]. Yet, we are routinely forced to do this work in silos, repeatedly reinventing the wheel because the connective tissue of our sector remains unfunded. Critical Frequency is designed to change this. 

We are building an architecture whose foundation is The Commons: a shared digital infrastructure and community-controlled archive of Black queer British cultural memory [2]. Through this substrate, we are commissioning an openly licensed fair-use and image-rights framework, alongside comprehensive cooperative governance documentation [2]. This ensures that the structural apparatus we build becomes shared sector property [2]. Any peer organisation across the European Black queer and structurally excluded sector will be able to adopt these frameworks, templates, and archive protocols without having to repeat the costly legal and infrastructural research [2]. We are doing the heavy lifting to create a shared blueprint for community ownership, designed to be inherited [3].

**Building Substrate, Not Competing for Funding**

When a new, large-scale proposition enters our ecosystem, it is entirely correct to ask: *"Is this scope creep? What's in it for us?"* We want to be unequivocally clear. BLKOUT is not competing for your funding, and we are not encroaching on your remit. Your remit stays yours; the substrate gets stronger. 

We are explicitly not building a parallel service to compete with existing peer organisations. We are building the foundational apparatus—the relational and digital substrate—that all of our work can stand on [4]. To ensure this does not drain the diversified resourcing landscape we all rely upon, we are seeking a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years to underwrite this infrastructure [5]. We will not reframe this ask to assemble a panel of contributing funders; complex systems change requires a single, unified cornerstone partner to catalyse the integration [5]. We are drawing new, structural investment into the ecosystem, rather than fighting over the existing scarcity. Crucially, this entire architecture is community-led from the start—a non-negotiable structural reality that we categorically refuse to soften into merely being "designed with community input."

**Solidarity Infrastructure Over Prevention Economics**

We are building this because the systems meant to serve us are broadcasting on the wrong frequencies [6]. The clinical and diagnostic establishment cannot hear what it cannot recognise, and it cannot treat what it cannot see [6]. 

The systemic data void regarding Black queer men is profound. Triangulated analysis suggests that between 150 and 200 Black queer men are detained under the Mental Health Act annually in England, at an estimated cost to the NHS of £12–16 million [1]. It is vital to cite these figures with their original caveats intact: this estimate is indicative because no official dataset in England links Mental Health Act detention records to sexual orientation [7, 8]. The calculation must account for the systemic under-disclosure of sexuality, it cannot perfectly map the non-linear compounding of intersectional discrimination, it does not capture geographic variation, and it must factor in the severe trust barriers that prevent our communities from presenting early to clinical services [8, 9]. 

While these economic figures are stark, our case for this work does not land through the prevention economics of the state. It lands through the necessity of solidarity infrastructure. Distress travels through our relationships, and therefore healing must travel through our relationships too [10, 11]. Our mechanism is operational network science [12]. We are strategically resourcing 1,825 culturally competent therapy recipients across the UK [12]. These individuals are chosen for their position as peripheral connectors and wide bridges within existing community clusters [12-14]. They will function as more than 45,000 network exposure points, mathematically designed to push our community past the 25 per cent tipping point where collective norms of wellness and help-seeking replace isolation [15, 16]. We do not need to build community from scratch; we need to properly resource the networks of care that already exist [10].

**Reparations and Structural Non-Extraction**

We are explicit with potential partners about the moral and economic nature of this funding. This work is grounded in the framework of UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147 (Basic Principles and Guidelines on the Right to a Remedy and Reparation) [17]. This resolution establishes unequivocally that gross violations of human rights create binding obligations to restore, compensate, rehabilitate, acknowledge, and prevent repetition [17]. 

When the mental health crises within our communities are the direct result of colonialism’s ongoing violence—manifesting through racialised policing, systemic neglect, and a cultural erasure that turns visibility into a mortal risk—the resourcing of our healing cannot be categorised as charity [17]. We categorically refuse the softening of this reality into the language of "social investment" or "impact philanthropy". This is debt repayment [17]. A funder entering this space is participating in a serious reparative project, building the infrastructure the state was obligated to build, but did not [18].

Furthermore, to guarantee that this infrastructure remains permanently non-extractive, BLKOUT Creative Ltd has been registered as a Community Benefit Society (CBS) with the UK Financial Conduct Authority (Reg. RS009639) [19]. We hold a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [19]. We will absolutely not soften this structural commitment: every penny of revenue is legally bound to community reinvestment; it cannot be captured or accumulated as private wealth [19]. This cooperative legal home ensures that the wealth generated by the community remains within the community.

**Holding Genuine Uncertainty as Strength**

Because we are addressing complex, intergenerational systemic failures, we must hold our genuine uncertainties not as risks to be mitigated, but as our greatest strengths. We openly acknowledge that network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept [20]. Community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale [20]. The intersection between artificial intelligence, network mapping, and community trust remains uncharted territory [20]. We refuse to soften these realities into a "tested methodology"—they are precisely the innovative design questions that make this solidarity work essential [20]. 

Therefore, any cornerstone partner must be prepared to engage with us in a rigorous action-learning frame [21]. We will not convert this transparent practice of collective inquiry into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure [21]. The partner will sit alongside the community to transparently examine the work as it evolves, without steering the fundamental direction of our governance [21].

**Concrete Commons Offers: The Advisory Circle and Free Ticketing**

Ultimately, Critical Frequency is an integrated architecture of six interlocking pieces [22]. Alongside the core programme (the spine), we are building *Seen/Scene* (a cultural curation room operating outside algorithmic engagement rankings) and *The Channel* (a broadcast layer redistributing Black queer creator content) [23, 24]. But it is the final two pieces of the architecture that serve as our most immediate, concrete offers to you, our peer organisations.

First, **The Advisory Circle** forms our relational substrate [4]. This is a standing circle of community organisers drawn from across the UK Black queer and adjacent structurally excluded movements [4]. Paid honoraria, this circle holds real authority over the action-learning curriculum, the evolution of the openly licensed governance documentation, and the broader practice [4]. The Advisory Circle is open. It deliberately distributes authority beyond BLKOUT, ensuring that the design questions and the learning are held collectively by the sector [4]. 

Second, we are launching a **Free Event Ticketing** service as tangible solidarity infrastructure [25]. Attached to our events calendar, this service is available to Black queer and adjacent community organisers across the UK [25]. It removes one of the constant, small extractions from community organising: the platform fees that drain resources from events they did nothing to create [25]. This service deepens our calendar from a mere listing into functional infrastructure, generating reciprocity without coercion [25]. Because BLKOUT is asset-locked, this service can grow alongside the sector without ever being captured by private interests [25].

We are formalising a methodology for survival that our lineage has practiced for centuries [17, 26]. We are not asking to compete with you. We are inviting you to utilise The Commons, to join the Advisory Circle, and to leverage the free ticketing infrastructure. Your remit stays yours; we are simply building the substrate so that, together, we can retune the system.
