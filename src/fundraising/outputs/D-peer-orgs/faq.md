# faq-D-peer

**Audience: Profile D — Black, queer, equity peer organisations**

---

**Q1: Is this scope creep? Are you moving into our operational space?**
We know how frustrating it is when a new programme encroaches on existing community work. To be clear, we are not building parallel services to replace what you already do so well. Your remit stays yours. Critical Frequency is about building the substrate—the shared digital and relational infrastructure—that all of our work can stand on securely [1]. We are doing the structural heavy lifting to create an openly licensed apparatus that the whole sector can inherit [2]. When the substrate gets stronger, everyone’s capacity to deliver their own mission grows.

**Q2: Are you competing with us for the same limited sector funding?**
No. We are deliberately seeking a single cornerstone partner to commit £4–6 million over five to seven years to underwrite this infrastructure [3]. We categorically refuse to assemble a panel of contributing funders for this founding tranche, as that drains the diversified resourcing landscape our entire ecosystem relies upon. Furthermore, we explicitly frame this investment as reparations under UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147, not "social investment" or "impact philanthropy" [4, 5]. We are drawing new, reparative capital into our ecosystem to pay a structural debt, rather than fighting over existing scarcity.

**Q3: What exactly is 'The Commons' you are building?**
The Commons is the shared digital infrastructure sitting underneath our work [1]. It functions as a community-controlled archive of Black queer British cultural memory, ensuring our history is safely held [1]. More practically for peer organisations, it houses an openly licensed fair-use and image-rights framework drafted by commissioned arts and IP lawyers [1]. It turns our operational architecture into shared sector property. Any organisation in our shared lineage can utilise these frameworks, templates, and archive protocols without having to repeat the costly legal and infrastructural research themselves [1].

**Q4: How does my organisation actually use the openly-licensed governance documents?**
As part of The Commons, we are writing up our Community Benefit Society (CBS) practice into a shareable, openly licensed cooperative governance template [1]. If your organisation wants to transition to a more democratic, member-owned model, you won't need to pay lawyers to build the foundational architecture from scratch. You can simply take our templates—covering democratic decision-making and asset-lock structures—and adapt them for your own needs [1]. It is designed to be fully adoptable across the wider European Black queer and structurally excluded sector, saving you significant time and money [1].

**Q5: Who decides what goes into the Commons archive?**
Because this infrastructure must be community-led from the start—which we absolutely refuse to soften into merely being "designed with community input"—decisions are not centralised within BLKOUT's core team [6, 7]. The Commons is a community-controlled archive [1]. We are developing the protocols for this shared stewardship through a two-year action-learning programme resourced by the Weaving Liberation Digital Justice Fund [7]. This process involves peer organisers working together to figure out how we ethically hold our own data, ensuring the archive is built through commoning, not just publishing [7].

**Q6: What is the Advisory Circle and what does it actually do?**
The Advisory Circle is the relational substrate of this entire architecture [7]. It is a standing circle of community organisers who hold real, practical authority over our action-learning curriculum, the listening-room toolkit, and the ongoing evolution of our openly licensed governance documentation [7]. While the CBS board holds formal legal governance, the Advisory Circle holds the practice [7]. It has a defined remit and a transparent budget [7]. It exists to deliberately distribute authority beyond BLKOUT, ensuring the sector has a direct, authoritative voice in how the infrastructure operates [2, 7].

**Q7: Can our staff sit on the Advisory Circle?**
Yes, absolutely. The Advisory Circle is deliberately drawn from across the UK Black queer and adjacent structurally excluded movements [7]. We want peer organisers from organisations like yours in the room. Crucially, participation is respected as labour: members of the Advisory Circle are paid honoraria for their time and expertise [7]. We are not asking your staff to volunteer their lived experience for our benefit. The circle meets at a sustainable cadence to ensure it doesn't lead to burnout, embedding cross-sector solidarity into the very fabric of how the programme is run [7].

**Q8: How does the Advisory Circle differ from a traditional steering group?**
We will strictly not convert this practice into a conventional, risk-averse steering-group structure. Traditional steering groups often act as institutional gates, managing risk and rubber-stamping predetermined decisions. The Advisory Circle operates within a rigorous action-learning frame [7, 8]. This means they sit with us to transparently examine the work as it evolves, asking difficult questions and holding authority over the curriculum [7]. It is about genuine participation and collective inquiry, ensuring that as we learn what works (and what doesn't), that intelligence is immediately shared across the sector [9].

**Q9: You mention holding 'genuine uncertainties'. Isn't that risky for the sector?**
We view our genuine uncertainties as our greatest methodological strengths, and we refuse to soften them into a "tested methodology" to appease funders [8]. Network-positioned therapy is a novel clinical concept [8]. Community-led data stewardship is unproven at this scale [8]. The interaction between AI and community trust remains uncharted territory [8]. By holding these uncertainties openly within the Advisory Circle's action-learning frame, we take on the risk of prototyping [8]. When we find the answers, the entire sector inherits the proven templates through The Commons without having to take the initial risks themselves.

**Q10: What does the CBS asset-lock mean for peer organisations?**
BLKOUT Creative Ltd is registered with the Financial Conduct Authority (Reg. RS009639) as a Community Benefit Society holding a permanent, unalterable asset-lock [10, 11]. We will never soften this structural commitment to non-extraction [10]. For you, this means every penny of revenue generated by our shared infrastructure is legally bound to community reinvestment [10]. It is structurally impossible for the wealth generated by this work to be captured or accumulated as private profit [10]. This guarantees that The Commons remains a permanent, protected resource for the structurally excluded sector forever.

**Q11: How does the Free Ticketing service work for us?**
This is a piece of tangible solidarity infrastructure available right now [12]. We are attaching a free ticketing service to BLKOUT’s events calendar, open to Black queer and adjacent community organisers across the UK [12]. Every time you use platforms like Eventbrite, they extract a percentage of your ticket fees, draining resources from events they didn't create [12]. Our service removes this extraction entirely [12]. Because of our CBS asset-lock, this service can scale alongside your events without ever being captured by private interests, generating real reciprocity without any coercion [10, 12].

**Q12: Why insist on 'reparations' language when talking to funders about shared infrastructure?**
We are building infrastructure to address crises stemming directly from colonialism’s ongoing violence, racialised policing, and cultural erasure [4]. Resourcing this healing is debt repayment, not charity [4]. We strictly anchor this in UN General Assembly Resolution 60/147, which establishes that gross human rights violations create binding obligations to restore and compensate [4]. By refusing to soften this into "impact philanthropy," we dismantle the deficit framing that paints our communities as problems [5]. It positions all of us—the entire sector—as rightful claimants receiving restitution, fundamentally changing the power dynamic with funders [5].

**Q13: How do you ensure this remains community-led from the start?**
Too often, programmes are "designed with community input" but ultimately controlled by centralised institutions. We reject that model. Being community-led from the start is hardwired into our architecture [7]. Legally, it is ensured by our CBS structure, which gives voting rights to our 200+ members [10]. Operationally, it is ensured by the Advisory Circle, which places authority over the practice directly into the hands of peer organisers from across the movement [7]. The community owns the cooperative, and the sector shapes the practice. Authority is permanently distributed, not concentrated [2].

**Q14: How does the action-learning process actually generate shared tools?**
The action-learning frame ensures that we don't just build tools in a vacuum and publish them. Through our Weaving Liberation Digital Justice Fund work, we are structuring the development of The Commons as a collaborative curriculum with peer organisers [7]. Because the way you build a commons is to actively "common," the Advisory Circle works through the practical challenges of archiving and governance together [7]. The solutions we arrive at are then formalised into the openly licensed templates [1]. You get tools that have been rigorously tested by your peers, built for our specific realities.

**Q15: When will these Commons tools be ready for us to use?**
We are structuring this through a highly specified Foundation Year (Year 1), which requires an upfront £250,000 to £500,000 commitment from our cornerstone partner [13]. This year is dedicated to the concrete construction of the trust and operational tooling [13]. During this time, the Advisory Circle is convened, the digital platform for The Commons is specified, and the openly licensed documentation programme begins [13]. The Free Ticketing service launches immediately [13]. By the close of Year 1, the shared infrastructure will be operationalised and ready for the wider sector to begin adopting [13, 14].
